Introduction¶

Sorting is defined as any process of arranging items systematicaly. It has two common, yet distinct meanings. The first is ordering, or arranging items in a sequence ordered by some criterion. The second is categorizing, i.e. grouping items with similar properties. Sorting encompasses the rearrangement of numbers (or other orderable objects) in a collection (or array) into their correct (numeric or lexographical) order. This entails arranging the array of elements according to certain pre-established ordering rules [28]. In computer science, arranging in an ordered sequence is what defines sorting. For example, when large amounts of data are to be sorted, efficiency can become an issue. For clarity, it should be stated that the opposite of sorting, is shuffling, i.e. rearranging a sequence of elements in a random and ostensibly meaningless order [17]. Sorting is a common operation in many applications and many efficient algorithms to perform such tasks have been developed. For example, some of the most common uses of sorted sequences are:

  • Making a lookup or search efficient.
  • Making merging of sequences efficient.
  • Enable processing of data in a defined order.

Because of the extreme importance of sorting in almost all computer algorithm and database applications, a great deal of effort has been expended in the creation and analysis of efficient sorting algorithms. Many computations and tasks become much easier by correctly sorting data in advance. Indeed, a huge portion of research conducted in the early years of computing was focused primarily on sorting data collections that were far too large for those primitive computers to store in memory. Nowadays, the scale of data sets being processed has reached an order of terabytes [3].

One of the main goals of sorting algorithms is to rearrange the elements so that they are ordered from smallest to largest, or to generate a new copy of the inputted sequence with such an order [1]. Sorting encapsulates the process of arranging or ordering a collection of elements such that each item and its successor satisfy a prescribed relationship [2]. A collection of elements is said to be sorted provided each element is less that or equal to its successor. In addition, if duplicate elements are present, they must be contiguous on the subsequent ordered collection. The elements of the collection should also be the same before and after sorting, this is known as stable sorting.

Whether we are aware of it or not, we encounter many examples of sorting in everday life. For example, the listings of a phone book, the definitions in a dictionary, terms in an index, or a list of cities by zip code or population count, rank of college point scores, list entries of a bank statement by date. Moreover, many computational tasks become more parsimonious by correctly sorting infomation in advance. For example, finding whether duplicates exist in a data set, determining the frequency (occurence) of each distinct element and so on. Indeed, sorting is among the most important and well studied of all computing problems. In fact, it is a problem in itself, not merely a pre-processing step. This fact has resulted in a huge amount of sorting algorithm research and in the genesis of many different algorithms. Indeed, oftentimes, data sets are stored in an ostensibly unsorted order. Nevertheless, a data analyst should be able to typically rely on calls to built-in or custom made sorting functions as it is rare to have special enough circumstances to warrant implementing a sorting algorithm from scratch [2]. These facts herald the need to better understand the operations involved in various different sorting algorithms. Indeed, there are many different sorting algorithms and even more ways in which they can be implemented [20].

Time & space complexity:

How much time do we need to solve a problem and how much resources do we need?

These questions are arguably two of the most important questions in programming and data science. In fact, it can be posited that the entire field of algorithmic science has been devoted to reducing time (execution time) and space (memory) complexity required for solving a particular problem. In order to reduce the time required, we must first measure the time required. Execution time of a sorting algorithm is contingent upon a number of factors including size of input, whether elements are pre-sorted to any extent, distribution of elements, system configuration, RAM and so on. For now, we will devote our concerns to a single crucial factor: the size of input.

  • Worst-case analysis:

The worst case analysis anticipates the greatest amount of running time that an algorithm needs to solve a problem for any input size n. This genre gives an upper bound on the computational complexity. Significantly, it also guarnatees that the performance of the algorithm will not get worse. In general, we consider the worst case performance very often [20].

  • Best-case analysis:

Best case anticipates the least amount of running time necessary for an algoithm to solve a problem for any input of size n. Here, the running time gives us a lower bound on the complexity. Most analysts do not consider the best case for the reason that it is not especially useful to do so [20].

  • Average-case analysis:

Generally, the average case is considered approximately as bad as the worst case time. That said, it is often used to review the performance of an alorithm if we average out its behaviour over all possible sets of input data. It is also much more difficult to carry out the process of analysis and typically demands considerable mathematical refinement. For this reason, worst case analysis has become so prevalent [20].

The majority of algorithms in use have an efficiency of either O(n2) or O(n log n). One way to recognize the best suitable algorithm for a given problem is to implement both algorithms and determine their efficiencies, most importantly the running time of a function. If the observed run time matches the predicted running time of the analysis and also outperforms the other algorithm, then the faster would intuitively be the better choice to apply to the given problem. Most algorithms perform very well on small input arrays and take longer for larger arrays. Typically, the time taken by the algorithm grows with the size of the input, so by convention we should describe the running time of the algorithm as a function of the size of its input. This phenomena is known as the growth rate or growth order of a function [20].

Growth order table and chart [21]

The growth rate of an algorithm is ultimately the rate at which the cost of the algorithm grows as the size of input grows. This fact allows us to compare the relative performance of different algorithms. For example, when the input size n increases to a greater extent, Merge sort with its n log n worst case running time overcomes Insertion sort whose worst case time is n2. This means that Merge sort is asymptotically more efficient than Insertion sort [20].

The running time of an algorithm for a specific input also depends on the number of operations executed. The greater the number of operations, the longer the running time of an algorithm. We usually want to know how many operations an algorithm will execute in proportion to the size of its input, which by convention we call n. For example, a sorting algorithm is said to run in linear time if its time execution is directly proportional to the input size, i.e. time grows linearly as input size n increases. In addition, the execution time of certain sorting algorithms is strongly related to the number of inversions in the input instance. Here, the number of inversions in a collection is a measure of how far away it is from being sorted. For example, the list [5,4,7,3] has four inversions (5,4), (5,3), (4,3) and (7,3). This is because all of these pairs are still "to be sorted". We will explore more about each of the individual algorithm's time complexities below, but for now it is enough to know that algorithms should have good run-time efficiency in the best, average or worst case scenarios.

Sorting algorithms also have different memory requirements, i.e. space complexity, depending on how that algorithm works. Furthermore, if an algorithm uses only a fixed additional quantity of working space, irrespective of input size, then it is said to be "in-place". This is a desirable quality in an algorithm particularly memory availability is a concern. Properties of the sorting algorithm should also be well-suited to the class of input instances which are expected. Here we must always consider the advantages and disadvanatges when choosing a sorting algorithm.

In-place sorting:

As previously mentioned, sorting algorithms may require some extra space for comparison and temporary storage of few data elements. These algorithms do not require any extra space and sorting is said to happen in-place or for example, within the array itself. This is called in-place sorting. Bubble sort which we will see below is a prime example of this. That said, with some algorithms, the script requires space which is more than or equal to the elements being sorted. Sorting which uses equal or more space like this is called not-in-place or out-place sorting. Merge sort is an example of this which we will also see below [11]. The in-place algorithms are typically slower than the algorithms which use additional memory [20].

Stable sorting:

Algorithms should also be stable, that is the process of preserving the relative ordering of elements which are equally valued i.e. the order of the input is preserved in the directly sorted output. Therefore, if a sorting algorithm, after sorting the contents, does not change the sequence of similar content in which they appear, it can be classed as stable. Conversely, if a sorting algorithm, after sorting the contents changes the sequence of similar content in which they appearm it is classed as unstable. Stability matters when we wish to maintain the sequence of original elements like in a tuple for example [11].

Algorithm Adaptivity

Another important property of sorting algorithms is whether or not they are adaptive. An algorithm is said to be adaptive if it takes advantage of already "sorted" elements that are to be sorted. That is, while sorting, if the input array has some element already sorted, adaptive algorithms take this into account and will therefore not attempt to re-order them. A non-adaptive algorithm is one which doesn't take this factor into account i.e. it doesn't consider elements which are aleady sorted. Instead they try to force each element to be re-ordered to confirm their sortedness [11].

Comparator functions:

When sorting collections of elements, it be necessary to employ a custom ordering scheme. In many cases, we have to compare components of a function for example:

  • if x < y, return True
  • if x > y, return False
  • if x = y, break

Sorting algorithms are independent of the comparator function e.g. "less than " which is to be employed. Thus, we do not place much concern with the specific details of the comparator function employed when designing sorting algorithms. Comparator functions in an algorithm can make them more effective and even increase their time efficiency. For example, to sort a collection Z, the elements of Z must be reorganised such that Z[i] < Z[j] then i < j. This means that given two index positions i and j, the value at Z i.e. index i, is less than the array value at index j if the value index i is less than the index j. Here, the addition of the comparator function makes the algorithm more effective [15].

In general, there are two types of operations performed within sorting algorithms; comparison operations and assignment operations. For example, to sort an array, the algorithm must compare each element in that array to determine the appropriate position in the final sorted array, i.e. comparison operations. This affects the overall running time due to the fact that if the input size grows, then the number of comparisons must also increase. Conversely, with assignment operations, each time the desired position is found, the algorithm needs to swap the element by using a temporary variable. This too affects running time. However, when compared to a comparison operation, the influence of an assignment operation on running time is significantly less [20].

Classification:

Sorting algorithms are predominantly classified by the general method they use to sort an array. The simplest and most intuitive way to do this is to compare the elements in the array (or list) with each other and organize them according to their relative order, moving an item forward of backwards in the array depending on whether adjacent items are greater or smaller. This is call comparison sort. Non-comparison sorts are the other core type of sorting algorithm. Another way to classify algorithms is whether they are in-place or recursive [27].

Comparison sorts:

A comparison sort algorithm is a type that only reads the list elements through a single abstract comparison operation such as "less than or equal to" comparator (or a three-way comparison) that determines which of two elements should occur first in the final sorted array [16]. The only requirement is that the operator forms a "total preorder" over the data i.e. by comparing a pair of elements at a time via the order ≤ [15]. Types of comparison sort algorithms include Insertion, Bubble and Heap sort, and we will see examples of these below. A good way to think about comparison sorts is that of someone who has a set of unlabelled weights and a balance scales. The aim here is to line up the weights in order by their weight without any information except that obtained by placing two weights on the scale to determine which one is heavier, of if they weigh the same.

There are some limits placed on the performance of comparison sorts. Firstly, a comparison sort must have an average case lower bound of n log n, i.e. linearithmic time. This is a consequence of the limited information available though comparisons alone. Non-comparison sorts on the other hand, can acheive O(n) i.e. linear time by using operations other than comparisons, allowing them to sidestep this lower bound [16]. Futhermore, it can be proved that a sorting algorithm that involves comparing pairs of values can never have a worst-case time better than O(n log n), where n is the size of the array to be sorted. However, under some special conditions having to do with the values to be sorted, it is possible to design other kinds of sorting algorithms that have better worst-case times, such as Counting sort or Bucket sort (non-comparison sorts) [15].

Non-comparison sorts:

Due to the working difference between these two types of algorithms, generally, the non-comparison based sorting algorithms like Counting Sort and Bucket Sort are typically faster than comparison based types such as Merge Sort and Heap Sort. Non-comparison types such as these are not limited to n log n time. Non-Comparison sorting algorithms use the internal characters to rearrange the values of an array into the correct order. Advanced recursive algorithms like Merge sort or Heap sort are difficult to implement, but nevertheless run more efficiently on an array of data, typically in O(n log n) steps. In addition, while a "sort key" is used with comparison sorts i.e. the specially selected data used to make the comparison, with non-comparative sorting, the keys are not compared. Instead, they algorithm sorts the keys by sorting individual elements which share the same position, as we will see.

Recursive and Non-recursive sorts:

A recursive algorithm means that it calls itself with smaller input values and obtains the result for the current input by applying simple operations to the returned value for the smaller input. Typically the problem is solved by utilizing solutions to smaller variants of the same problem, and the smaller variants reduce to an easily solvable instance, then one can employ a recursive algorithm to solve that problem. For example, Merge sort is recursive while Insertion sort is non-recursive, since it does not follow these steps [20].

Criteria for choosing a sorting algorithm: [3]

Each algorithm carries it own advantages and disadvantages. For example, Bubble sort is effective enough to sort a small number of items, whereas for a large number of items Quick sort typically performs better. In general, simple sorting algorithms perform two operations such as compare and assign one element. These operations proceed over and over until the data is sorted [20]. By contrast, more advanced algorithms like Merge or Heap rely on tree structures for their comparison operations. Sorting Algorithms¶

1. A simple comparison-based sort: Insertion Sort¶

Insertion sort is simple comparison algorithm. It is iterative in type and works by splitting an array of size n into a head (sorted) and tail (unsorted) subarray. It is similar in method to the way that we sort cards, and it is useful to think about it in that way.

You're holding the cards in your hand, and they are sorted. The dealer now hands you one new card. By convention, you have to put it in its correct place so that you cards are still sorted. This card could be smaller than some of the cards you're already holding, so you go down through the cards comparing the new card against each card until you find its right place. Now you "insert" the new card in this place. Again, the dealer gives you another new card and you repeat the procedure, ad- infinitum until he stops giving you cards. This is the idea behind Insertion sort. It loops over the array indexes (positions) starting from the left (index [1]). Each new position is analogous to the new card you were given, and you need to insert it in its correct position in the "sorted" subarray to the left of that position. Insertion sort is relatively easy to implement, stable, in-place and very effective at sorting small arrays, albeit, it can be somewhat inefficient on random lists [4].

Time & space complexity:

Insertion is an iterative algorithm in which it requires n-1 iterations of n elements. It is also an example of an algorithm where the best and worst cases are different [2]. In the worst case scenario, if the elements are in reverse order, the computational complexity would be quadratic i.e. O(n2). However, in the best case, where each of the n elements is in its correct place, Insertion sort takes linear time or O(n). In fact, Insertion sort is the only comparison-based algorithm that exibits this quality. The efficiency of Insertion sort increases when duplicate elements are present, since there are fewer swaps to perform. Unfortunately, Insertion is too conservative when all n elements are distinct and the array is randomly organised. In the average of worst cases, each of the n elements must be transposed a linear number of positions and thus it requires O(n2) quadratic time [3].

One advantage of Insertion is that it requires very little extra space to function. It only needs to reserve space for a single element, i.e. O(n) space complexity. Like Bubble, Insertion sort is inefficient for large arrays. Nevertheless it still works twice as effectively as Bubble sort. For the reason that it is simple and highly efficient on small arrays, it can be used within some other sophisticated algorithms like Merge sort for greater efficiency.

Procedure:

With Insertion sort, we begin from the left of the array and set the key at index 1. Here, the function begins by assuming the first item is already sorted in its correct position. Next, an iteration is performed over the remaining values so that each element can be inserted into its correct position within the sorted portion of the array. The sorted portion of the array is at the left or front while the yet-to-be-sorted portion are at the end. The "index" loop index variable (see code below), denotes the separation point between the two parts. The inner while loop is used to find the insertion point (position) within the sorted array and simultaneously, shifts the elements down to make room for the next element. In this way, the inner loops starts from the end of the sorted subarray and works its way to the front. After finding the correct position, the element is inserted.

# Insertion Sort [22]:

def insertionSort(alist):
   for index in range(1,len(alist)): # loop set from index [1] to end of array length

     currentvalue = alist[index] # Temporary variable set for current index array element
     position = index # Denotes the separation point between the two parts

     while position>0 and alist[position-1]>currentvalue: # While loop finds the insertion point/position within the sorted array
         alist[position]=alist[position-1]
         position = position-1 # Shifts the elements down to make room for the next element

     alist[position]=currentvalue # Assigning current value variable to new (sorted) position in alist array

2. An efficient comparison-based sort: Merge Sort¶

Merge sort goes back as far as 1945 when it was devised by John von Neumann. It employs recursion with an algorithmic paradigm known as the divide-and-conquer approach. Here, we break the problem into subproblems that are similar to the original problem. We solve the subproblems recursively, then combine the solutions of the subproblems to solve the original problem, i.e. we divide, conquer and then combine. This pattern consists of three steps [1]:

  • Divide: If the input size is smaller than a certain threshold (e.g. one or two elements), solve the problem directly using a straightforward method (Base case) and return the solution so obtained. Otherwise, divide the input data into two more disjoint subsets.
  • Conquer: Recursively solve the subproblems within the subsets
  • Combine: Take the solutions to the subproblems and merge them into a solution to the original problem.

Time & space complexity:

Merge sort offers O(n log n) in the worst case, and can be used to efficiently store data that is stored externally in a file. In this way it allows for sorting with extra storage and therefore facilitates more effiecient space complexity. Any merge operation must take time that is linear in the total size of the two arrays in the worst case, because every element must be checked in order to determine the correct order. In fact, Merge sort has a run time of only O(n log n) in all cases. Merge is an efficient sorting method compared to even sophisticated methods such as Heap sort. However, it is considered inefficient for the reason that it requires twice the number of additional memory for the second array than other sophisticated algorithms [20].

The algorithm must complete the “merge” phase in O(n) time after recursively sorting the left and right half of the range “arr” (start, end) (see code below), placing the correctly ordered elements in the array. Because the "copy" is a true copy of the entire array, the terminating base cases of the recursion will work because they referenced the original elements of the array directly at the respective index locations. It should be noted that this observation is a notably sophisticated one and is they key to the algorithm. Furthermore, the final merge step only requires O(n) operations which ensures the total benchmark performance stays at O(n log n). As mentioned, Merge is an external sorting algorithm, and as a result needs O(n) additional memory space for n elements. Because the copy array is the only extra space used by the algorithm, the total space requirements are O(n) [3]. Another disadvantage of Merge sort is that it is not recommended for smaller arrays because it operates recursively. That said, Merge has strong points. For example, even though it does not know the length of the array in the beginning in some cases, it only requires a single scan through the list to figure it out [20].

Merge sort is a very good all rounder. Nevertheless, it is not in-place because it has to make complete copies of the entire input array. Compare this to Insertion sort, which at any time keep an extra copy of only one array entry rather than copies of all the array entries (each element is copied O(n log n) times). Hence, if space is scarce, it might not be wise to use merge sort.

Procedure:

Because we're using a divide-and=conquer method to sort, we need to decide what our sub-problems are going to look like. The full problem is to sort the entire array correctly. We can think of the sub-problem as sorting the subarray at the first index p and going through to the last index r. We can say the subarray of the array is: array[p..r]. In terms of notation, for an array of n elements, we can say that the original(full) problem is to sort array[0..n-1] i.e. from index [0] to the last indexed element.

How does Merge sort implement divide-and-conquer?

  1. Divide by finding the number j of the position halfway (midpoint) between p and r. This is done by adding p and r, dividing by 2 and rounding down.
  2. Conquer by recursively sorting the subarrays in each of the sub-problems created by the divide step above, i.e. recursively sort the subarray array[p..r] and then recursively sort the subarray array[j+1..r].
  3. Combine by now "merging" both of the sorted subarrays back into the single subarray array[p..r].

The base case here is a subarray containing less than two elements, i.e. when p is greater than or equal to r, because a subarray with no or one elements is already sorted. Therefore, if the size of the array is 0 or 1, the Merge sort function will return itself immediately. Conversely, we divide-and-conquer only when p < r, i.e. we partition the two arrays of equal (or near equal) size and begin separately and recursively sorting the halves [13].

[2,3,6,7,9,11,12,14]

Great, but how exactly did array[0..3] get sorted? In precisely same way. This spliced array has more than two elements and it is therefore not a base case. So, here p = 0 and r = 3 which computes to j = 1, gets recursively sorted array[0..1] i.e. [14,7] and array[2..3] i.e. [3,12] resulting in array[0..3] i.e. [7,14,3,12]. Now we simply merge the first half with the second half resulting in [3,7,12,14].

Okay, but how did the subarray array[0..1] become sorted? With p = 0 and r = 1, we compute j = 0 and recursively sort array[0..0] i.e. [14] and array[1..1] i.e. [7] which results in array[0..1], still containing [14,7] and merge the first half with the second half thus producing [7,14]. 3. A non-comparison sort: Counting Sort¶

It is nearly impossible to do better than linearithmic runtime O(n log n). Indeed, but Counting sort facilitates the seemingly impossible by sorting a collection of elements in linear time (approximately). This is only possible due to the fact that the algorithm imposes assumptions about the type of input instances it can deal with.

Counting Sort was the brainchild of Harold H. Seward in 1954. Unlike merge sort which is comparison based, Counting sort is integer based. It’s a non-recursive, non-comparison algorithm and iterative in its implementation. It is based on keys between a specific range, i.e. between 0 and n. Counting sort assumes that the input consists of an integer in a small range which makes it faster. It works by counting the number of elements having distinct key values. It takes advantage of situations where we know the range of elements to be sorted and we know these elements are all integers. In a nutshell, it counts the number of distinct/unique elements and uses math to determine the sorted values. It can only be implemented on integers and this is also part of what limits its usability. It leverages the fact it knows the range of elements and that they are all integers and builds up a "count" array, which it employs to calculate how many numbers appear in the unsorted collection [6]. Counting sort also assumes that the input size of n has a non-negative integer key. While it affords an impressive runtime advantage, it comes with the disadvantage of lacking in more generalised applicabilty.

Time & space complexity:

Counting sort is O(n) in the best, worst, and average cases. It has a space complexity of O(k).

Even though we need some extra space for the count array, and we have to do some slightly complex operations on that count array, the overall amount of time that this algorithm takes is for the most part, linear. In other words, the time complexity of Counting sort depends directly on how many elements we have to sort. This also partly explains why the range and input size of our unsorted array matters. The space complexity hinges directly on the range of elements to be sorted. Indeed, we create a count array as a result of counting sort, and the larger the range, the larger the output will be. Thus, in some cases counting sort can be “bad news”. In truth, the space-time complexity of counting sort amounts to a combination of both the number of elements to be sorted n, and the range between the largest and smallest element or k. However, counting sort is typically only employed if k isn’t larger than n; in other words, if the range of input values isn’t greater than the number of values to be sorted. In that scenario, the complexity of counting sort is much closer to O(n), making it a linear sorting algorithm. Due to the fact Counting sort requires a duplicate array (and doesn’t always operate on the original array) it is classified as an out-of-place algorithm. Nevertheless, it does manage to maintain the order of elements as they appeared in the original array making it a stable algorithm. It also doesn’t require any external memory and can sort small amounts of data in internal memory [14]. One of the advantages of Counting sort is that is uses key values as indexes into an array. For this reason it can be incorporated into another sorting algorithm as a subroutine, hence it should maintain the relative order of elements with equal keys. On the other hand, it it not very suitable for large data sets or strings [20].

Procedure:

In our example we will have an array [1, 2, 9, 3, 2, 1, 4, 2, 3, 7, 1].

Notice that there are some duplicate elements, which is completely fine. Here, we know the data we're sorting is between range 1 and 9. We begin by creating the count array. We do this to do the subsequent work of calculating how many occurences of each number (between 1-9) we have in our input data. After we calculate the count array we begin sorting.

Once we have generated our count array, we can use the indices in the array to map to the elements themselves. When we initialize the count array, each index begins with an initial value of 0 i.e. like a reset counter. We thus need to populate the count array with the tallied elements for the unsorted input array. Here, we want to figure out how many times a single number appears in our input array. We do this until we get to the number 9, i.e. the last and maximum value in our input instance. This process of counting occurences is simply done by iteration, i.e. iterate and keep track of how many times we've seen a certain number, until we have iterated through the entire array.

We now count each element in the unsorted array, and keep track of the count at the index of the count array by incrementing the value of the index that maps on to the number. For example, the first element we start with is 1. We find index 1 in the count array which is currently set to 0 and increment it by one. Then we move to the next element i.e. 2, we again find the index (2) in the count array and increase it from 0 to 1. We do the same with all the input numbers. Essentially, as we iterate through the unsorted array, we count each element and keep track of that count in the corresponding index of the count array [14].

So we first determine the range of the input array, then we create a count array which is populated by tallying all the elements in the original array by number of occurence, in order to keep track of our count frequency. This can be represented by a histogram (as below). Here, we initialize the array count to count the number of occurences per key value/element, accumatively adding the values in the populated count array.

4. Another sorting algorithm of choice: Bubble sort¶

Bubble sort is based on the idea of repeatedly comparing pairs of adjacent elements and then swapping their positions if they exist in the wrong order. The algorithm rearranges the input values by iterating over the list multiple times, causing larger values to “bubble” to the top or end of the list. It is the simplest sorting algorithm, comparison based, but is not very suitable for large data sets. It is also a stable and in-place sorting algorithm. While it is simple and practical in some cases, it is slow for most problems. Indeed, Bubble sort is often considered the most inefficient sorting method since it must exchange elements before the final location is known. These “wasted” exchange operations are very costly. However, because Bubble sort makes passes through the entire unsorted portion of the list, it has the capability to do something most sorting algorithms cannot. If during a pass there are no exchanges, then we know that the list must be sorted. Bubble sort can be modified to stop early if it finds that the list has become sorted. This means that for lists that require just a few passes, Bubble sort can have an advantage in that it will recognize the sorted list and stop. The code example below includes this valuable modification, which is often referred to as the "Short Bubble" [7].

Time & space complexity:

Bubble sort yields O(n) in best case, and O(n2) in worst and average. To determine its efficiency, we must determine the total number of iterations performed by the inner loop for an array containing n values. The outer loop is executed n-1 times since the algorithm makes n-1 passes over the array. The number of iterations for the inner loop is not fixed, but depends on the current iteration of the outer loop. So, on the first pass over the array the inner loop executes n-1 times, on the second pass, n-2 times, on the third, n-3 and so on until it executed on the last pass. The total number of iterations for the inner loop will be the sum of the first n-1 integers which results in a run time of O(n2). As mentioned, Bubble sort is one of the most inefficient sorting algorithms due to the total number of swaps required. For example, given an array of elements in a reverse order, a swap is performed for every iteration of the inner loop which has a costly price. Even if the array is already sorted, with no need to sort it, the implementation will still perform all n2 iterations because it has no way of knowing the array is already sorted, unless of course the aforementioned modified Short Bubble is used instead [2]. Even though Bubble is considered the most inefficient algorithm, it still has some strong traits such as simplicity, ease of implementation and the ability to identify if the list is already sorted if it is correctly implemented. Bubble sort also has a respectable space complexity of O(1). That said, it can be inappropriate for large volumes of data elements and repetitive problems [20].

Procedure:

Bubble sort compares each element with its neighbouring element to the right, and swaps them if they are in the incorrect order. For example, in an unsorted array of 5 elements, start with the first two elements and sort them in ascending order. Here we compare the elements to check which one is of greater value. Next we compare the second and third element to determine which one is greater and sort them in ascending order. On the next iteration we compare the third and forth in the same way, and this is repeated until no more steps are required. Let's take a look [8]:

First we take an unsorted array for our example:

[14,33,27,35,10]

Bubble sort begins with the very first two elements, comparing them to determine which one is greater.

[14,33,27,35,10]

In this case, 33 is greater than 14 so it is already sorted. Next we move on to the next pair of 33 and 27. We know that 27 is less than 33 and thus these two elements must be swapped:

[14,27,33,35,10]

Now we reach 35 and 10. We know 10 is less than 35 and hence they are not sorted. We therefore swap these values. We have reached the end of the array. After one iteration the array looks like this:

[14,27,33,10,35]

Now we start the second iteration as we know the array is not fully sorted yet. After the second iteration the array looks like this:

[14,27,10,33,35]

Then a third:

[14,10,27,33,35]

Notice how after each iteration is finished, at least one value moves to the end. This feature is what gave the algorithm its name i.e. larger values "bubble up" to the end during sorting. Iteration continues until no swap is required whereby Bubble sort "learns" that the array is completely sorted:

[10,14,27,33,35]

def shortBubbleSort(alist):
    exchanges = True # Sets exchanges initially to True
    passnum = len(alist)-1 # Passnum is the temporary holder of second from last value of array length
    while passnum > 0 and exchanges: # As long as passnum variable is greater than 0 then...
       exchanges = False # Don't swap, and...
       for i in range(passnum): # For i in range (4 in this case)
           if alist[i]>alist[i+1]: # If i is greater than the adjacent element (i+1) then... 
               exchanges = True    # Swap their positions
               temp = alist[i]     # temp is the assigned temporary holder of i  
               alist[i] = alist[i+1] # Swap index [0] for index [1], e.g. in the first iteration of for loop
               alist[i+1] = temp # Swap value that is in temp holder with alist value, and shift up by one index position
       passnum = passnum-1 # passnum is swapped, i.e. shifts from value 4 to 3, e.g. to finish the first iteration of for loop

5. Another sorting algorithm of choice: Heap Sort¶

Heap sort sorts an array by first converting that array into a heap. It is a comparison based algorithm based exclusively on a binary heap data structure. Heap sort is similar to selection sort, in that we repeatedly find the largest element and place it at the end of the unsorted array. However, with heap, instead of iterating over the entire array, to find the largest element, we transform the array into a max heap to speed things up. Because we know that heaps must follow a specific order, we can leverage this property ad use it to find the largest element, and sequentially sort elements by selecting the root node of a heap and adding it to the end of the array, the root node being the current value at the top of the heap.

Time & space complexity:

When compared to other advanced sorting algorithms with the same complexity, Heap performs relatively slowly in real time. Nevertheless, it is usually considered more often for problems with large data sets [20]. Heap sort also achieves sorting status through far fewer swapped elements than that of Insertion sort. It has a worst case time complexity of O(n log n), a best case time complexity of O(n log n) and an average time complexity of O(n log n) as well. Thus, Heap sort is fast and scales well as n grows. Heap sort's space complexity is constant, i.e. O(1). Heap is also not a stable algorithm, and requires a constant space for sorting a list. When storing data in linked list data structures, Heap sort is not recommended due to the fact that it is very difficult to convert the linked list to a heap structure [20]. Despite this, heap sort is very fast and is widely used for sorting.

Procedure:

First, we build a max heap function from the input data. Next, once the largest element is at the root node of the heap (the top), and every parent node is larger than its children, we swap the largest element with the element at the end of the heap. Finally while the last element might be in the right place, the root node probably isn't, so we move down the root node element to its correct position using the heapify function [10].

What is a binary heap?

A Binary heap is a special type of binary tree. A binary tree follows certain rules. For example, it must always have a heap structure, where the overall levels of the tree are filled from left to right. Second, it must be either ordered by a max heap or a min heap. For example in a max heap, every parent node including the root is greater than or equal to the value of its children noded. Array elements are stored in a special order in the tree, such that the value in a parent node is greater or smaller than the values in its two children nodes. The parent is called the max heap and the children the min heap. Since a binary heap is a binary tree, it can be easily represented as an array. If the parent node is stored in index [i], the left child can be calculated by 2 x [i] + 1 and the right child by 2 x [i] + 2 (assuming indexing begins at [0]). We can see these calculations in the code below.

First we build a max heap from the input data. Here the largest element is stored at the root of the heap. Next we replace it with the last element of the heap followed by reducing the size of heap by 1. We then "heapify" the root of the tree. These steps are repeated while the size of the heap is greater than 1. Heapify can only be applied to a node if its child nodes are heapified. Thus, the heapication must be performed in bottom up order [9]:

So we have an unsorted input array. We need to take the array and convert it into a heap, in our case we will transform it into a max heap. This process is achieved by our initial "heapify" function coded below.

Once we have our array in a max heap format, we can be sure that the largest element is at root node of the heap. Even though the entire heap won't be sorted, every single parent node in our heap will be larger than its children. Thus, we'll move the largest value, located at the root (node) to the end of the heap by swapping it with the last element.

Now the largest heap element in the heap is located in the last node. We therefore know that it is in its correctly sorted position so it can be removed from the heap entirely. Now, we must make sure that the new root node element is in the correct position. It is highly improbable that the element we swapped into the root node position is in the correct place so we'll move down the root node element to its correct place, using the heapify function. The algorithm continues to repeat these steps until the heap is down to one single node. Here, we can see Heap sort's recursive style at work. At this point the algorithm "knows" that all the elements in the original unsorted array are now in sorted positions, and the last node element will logically end up being the first element in the sorted array [10].

Instead of treating the input as an array, we can treat it like nodes in a complete binary tree. So, the 0th element is the root; the 1st element in the array is the roots left child and so on.

We are treating the input like specific nodes in a tree. When we manipulate the tree by moving around elements or removing them, we are actually rearranging the underyling input array and not a separate tree structure. The tree is just to help with visualisation of how we interpret the array as a heap. In the computer's memory, it is an array that is being sorted.

We know that at the moment, the heap is not a valid one yet as for example, the largest element (9) isn't at the root, i.e. the node at the very top. So, to transform the tree into a heap (heapify) we work from the bottom of the tree upwards. We compare each node to its "children" and move nodes around so that the "parent" nodes are always larger than their children. This process causes smaller nodes to move lower down the tree bubbling down" to allow larger elements reach the top.

So, beginning at the lowest level i.e. 7, 9, 1, 4, we know that none of these values have children so we move up to the nodes above this level i.e. 3 and 2. First we can start with the left node (3) and its children (7 & 9). Because 7 and 9 are both greater than 3, we must move some elements around. Thus, we swap the 3 and the 9 to make the parent larger than its children. If we swapped 3 with 7 than we would encounter a problem as the parent (7) would be smaller than one of its children (9) and that violates the rules [12].

Now we examine the right node (2) and its children, since 4 is larger than 2 (and 1) we swap them.

Notice how now we already have two small valid max heaps, we're getting there.

Moving up a level now, we find we have 8 at the root. Because 8 is less than 9, 8 "bubbles down", and swaps places with the larger child 9.

Now we need to compare 8 to its children 7 and 3. Since 8 is larger than both of them we don't need to complete any additional swaps.

Repeatedly get the max from the heap:

Every time we remove an elements from the heap, it's the largest item in the array. Removing an item from the heap conveniently frees up space at the end of the underlying array where we can place the removed item (pay attention to the array as the tree changes).

First we remove the max element (step 2 & 3). This leaves a gap at the root that needs to be filled. So, we insert the right-most item there i.e. 2.

But now notice that this is no longer a valid heap. Our largest element (8) isn't at the root position, so we will "bubble down" to restore a heap (like in the heapify steps earlier) (diagram 5 & 6).

Notice how there is an empty index at the end of the array, we now fill that in with the 9 we removed. Now we have the largest item at the end of the array. We continue this process i.e. removing the next largest element in the heap and adding it to the growing sorted section at the end of the array:

Now, what happens to the next largest element...8? We remove it from the root and fill its spot with the bottom right-most element, i.e. 1. Then we must bubble down the 1 to make the heap valid once more.

As you can see from the diagrams, this process of working through and removing the largest values to put them at the end of the array and then bubbling down continues (i.e. 8 then 7 then 4 etc) until the array is fully sorted with the largest elements to the far right and decreasing in size as the array goes backward towards index zero on the furthest left [12].

Notice that large numbers are consequently “lifted up” in the resulting heap (which means they are swapped in the array with smaller elements to the left). Also, notice how the root element is always at the 0th index in the array and how the array becomes more and more sorted as the root pops off into its correct position and bubbling down continues. We can now see quite easily how we the process finishes itself now to result in a fully sorted array.

# Heap Sort [26]:

def heapify(arr, n, i): # array, n is size of heap, i is holder for largest element 
    largest = i # Initialize largest as root 
    l = 2 * i + 1     # Left = Multiplies root by 2 and adds one. Thus, if root = 7 then: 7 * 7 + 1 = 15 (l = 15) 
    r = 2 * i + 2     # Right = Multiplies root by 2 and adds two 
  
    # See if left child of root exists and is 
    # greater than root: 
    if l < n and arr[i] < arr[l]: # If l is less than n, and i is less than l then...
        largest = l               # assign l as largest (root)
  
    # See if right child of root exists and is 
    # greater than root: 
    if r < n and arr[largest] < arr[r]: # If r is less than n and largest less than r then... 
        largest = r                     # assign r as largest (root)
  
    # Change root, if needed: 
    if largest != i: # If root is not greater than i then...
        arr[i],arr[largest] = arr[largest],arr[i] # swap root 
  
        # Heapify the root. 
        heapify(arr, n, largest) # Make recursive call to heapify the root again
  
# Main function to sort an array of given size 
def heapSort(arr): 
    n = len(arr) # n equals length of array
  
    # Build a maxheap. 
    for i in range(n, -1, -1): # For i in range 
        heapify(arr, n, i) # Make a recursive call to heapify
  
    # One by one extract elements 
    for i in range(n-1, 0, -1): 
        arr[i], arr[0] = arr[0], arr[i] # Swap elements
        heapify(arr, i, 0) # Make recursive call to heapify

Pre-benchmarking preparation:

It is important to use the right benchmarking approach to attain accurate and reliable performance results. There are many ways by which an algorithm can be analyzed. To compute the performance of an algorithm we typically construct a suite of independent trials for which the algorithm is executed. Each trial is intended to execute an algorithm on an input size n. The suite is executed and millisecond level timings are taken before and after the observable operation [3].

The arrays of randomly generated integers were produced using Numpy's randint module. The rationale for choosing certain magnitudes of input sizes was driven by the goal of optimally demonstrating/testing the effects input size has on running time, as well as aiming to show each individual algorithms true growth signature. The input size array was manually created and took the form of a list named "lengths". Input sizes ranged from n = 50 to n = 10,000.

Input size array: lengths = [50,100,200,400,500,750,1000,2500,5000,10000]

Prior to implementation of the full benchmarking program, the system was prepared with a warm-up by means of feeding each algorithm separately with a random array, and running the algorithms without being timed. After this, the full integrated benchmarking program was ran in its entirety.

The option to use a random seed for generating random arrays for each algorithm iteration was inititally considered and tested. However, it was later felt more appropriate not to set a random seed. The reason being was that upon further consideration, it was inferred that only by iterating over a different random array each time, would it afford the optimal method of testing the capability of each algorithm, i.e. yield all-round better and more valid runtime measurements. In fact, it was found that when the algorithms were benchmarked using a random seed, some performed slightly better, some slightly worse, and others notably worse then when a seed was not set. For example, when Counting sort was measured at 5,000 and 10,000 elements with a set seed, it performed twice and three times faster than without a seed respectively, i.e. for 5,000 elements: 0,002.0ms (set seed) vs 0,006.0ms (not set), and for 10,000 elements: 0,003.0ms (set seed) vs 0,006.0ms (not set). Furthermore, Insertion sort typically performed faster when a seed was not set, Merge was relatively the same whether the seed was set or unset, and Bubble sort was slower when the seed was set. Interestingly, Heap sort showed the most notable difference of all algorithms when tested with a set seed. Here, Heap yielded a running time of 0,211.9ms on 5,000 elements (set seed) vs only 0,083.0ms (not set), and a time of 0,392.8ms on 10,000 elements (set seed) vs 0,197.9ms (not set). While these effects are intriguing, we cannot confidently make any tentative explanations as to why this was the case.

Typical growth order plot:

As previously described, the growth rate for an algorithm is the rate at which the cost of the algorithm grows as the size of its input grows. For example, the growth rate c or 0(1), describes a constant running time, i.e. an entirely constant horizontal line. The algorithm with logarithmic growth considers being best suited for many problems for the reason it requires the least amount of resources. Similarly, the algorithm with quadratic growth considers the worst case performance with greater running time equation, however better than cubic or exponential. Linear growth rate maintains that as size of input grows, the running time grows by the exact same proportion. The exponential growth rate function describes that the algorithm with a running time grows exponentially with the increase in input size n.

By comparing the multiple algorithm plot and the typical growth order plot, we can infer where each of our sorting algorithm outputs lie on the growth order plot and vice versa. This is still only an approximation and should be interpreted with some caution. It is interesting to compare the algorithmic performances with typical growth orders nonetheless, and we can see that our algorithms do in the most part, live up to their asymptotic reputations. For example, Bubble sort is well know for its worst case time of O(n2), i.e. quadratic, and that is what it appears to be displaying in this case. The same appears to be true of Insertion sort where it appears to display a slighly lower O(n2) growth pattern. Counting, Merge and Heap sort all appear to display O(log n) or perhaps O(n) time on the chart even though they are known for their linear and linearithmic best case times respectively. Interpretation and discussion:

Comparison of algorithms:

It seems the general consensus is that the efficiency of a sorting algorithm is contingent upon three factors, and sometimes also how effectively the algorithm was implemented under certain circumstances, and what type of machine was used. In order for a benchmarking to be classed as reliable, the analysis should aim to be unassociated with factors such as machine specification, programming style or running environment i.e. the software used to implement these processes. Nevertheless, the three core factors which should always be considered in an analysis are firstly to ensure efficiency via running time; secondly, the number of comparison and assignment operations performed, and lastly, the space, i.e. memory required for the program and the data used during running time. These are all computational resources necessary for implementation. The necessity of analysis is to determine which algorithm required fewer resources to solve the same given problem. These considerations afford a prudent approach to determining the optimal algorithm for any given problem [20].

Running times and effects of input sizes:

There are two crucial reasons to calculate the running time of an algorithm. One is to understand and appreciate how the time grows as a function with respect to its input parameters, the other is to compare two or more algorithms on how they cope with the same problem. Typically, the running time includes the number of memory accesses performed, the number of comparisons between integers and the number of times a loop (inner or outer) is executed. However, there are still other factors not related to algorithms perse that will affect the running time.

The empirical data obtained from the benchmarking process in this case revealed that the speed of each algorithm, from fastest to slowest for a sufficiently large array (n = 10000) ranked as follows:

1. Counting sort

2. Merge sort

3. Heap sort

4. Insertion sort

5. Bubble sort

In fact, this exact ranking order of algorithm performance is the same for all input sizes ranging from from n = 100 to n = 10,000. It is only where n = 50 are all the algorithms found to be relatively the same in terms of performance. Judging hy this pattern of prevalence, we can clearly see how exceptional Counting sort really is. By contrast, and to put it bluntly, Bubble sort is painfully slow when faced with large arrays. Indeed, once Bubble passes around 500 elements it really begins to crawl. Interestingly, when loaded with 50 elements, Bubble sort still slightly outperformed Counting sort (however this was not true when a random seed was used).

In general, the results from this benchmarking analysis were ostensibly consistent with each algorithms individual reputation and qualities regarding their individual growth orders. Results were approximately on par with what was expected and aimed for prior to and during analyses.

There is a very notable difference apparent in the time taken to sort very large arrays between the two fastest and three slowest algorithms. For example, all five algorithms perform very well and relatively identically on input sizes from 50 up to 500. Here we see very little difference in growth order. However, once they reach around 750 to 1000 elements, we start to notice that their individual growth properties diverge dramatically from one another. This phenomena is especially true with regards to Insertion and Bubble sort. However, Counting sort still remains extremely fast and unaffected right up until 10,000 input elements. This is quite astonishing.

Consistent with most previous benchmarking studies, Bubble sort was found to be significantly slower than all of the other algorithms across every single input size ranging from 50 to 10,000. This fact is reflected saliently in the multiple output plot below. The only time where Bubble is not slower than all other algorithms is when handling 50 elements. Bubble was also found to be over 4 times slower when it made the leap from n = 2,500 to n = 5,000. This difference is quite staggering considering an increase of elements of 100%, i.e. from 2,500 to 5,000, resulted in a running time of 400% more, i.e. from 1,807.0 milliseconds (ms) to 7,213.9ms. That said, Bubble's performance was quite acceptable until it reached 750 elements, after which we begin to see the true nature of its capabilities wane, which is strongly reflected in its running time. Indeed, when Bubble leaped from 5000 to 10,000 elements it took almost four times longer to process an increase of 100% elements (28,611.7ms).

Similarly, Insertion sort took over 3 times longer to sort the same elements when it stepped from 2,500 to 5,000 elements. Here, it jumped from 0,989.4ms to 3,187.2ms as n increased. Insertion performed admirably until it encountered 1,000 elements, and struggled significantly with 10,000 i.e. 13,011.6ms. These finding corroborate many previous observations that Insertion and Bubble sort are only really useful sorting relatively small arrays. Insertion was also found to be notably slower than Merge, Counting and Heap, but certainly nowhere near as slow as Bubble sort which is approximately twice as slow as Insertion on all input size instances. Nevertheless, we should remember that Insertion and Bubble are simple comparison sort which only really do well with smaller arrays. Compare this to efficient comparison sorts like Merge and Heap which are notably more advanced. For example, Merge and Heap both rely on tree structures to perform so efficiently, whereas Insertion and Bubble do not.

It is impossible not to admire how fast Counting sort performs. Indeed, this performance property becomes even more apparent as n increases. Running time remains almost entirely unscathed. For example, Counting's running time remains consistent at approximately 0,001.0ms right up until 1000 elements, when even then it only increases to a mere 0,001.0ms. Furthermore, even when the input feed is doubled notably in size, i.e. from 2,500 to 5,000 elements, Counting sort's running time only increases by 0,002.0ms to 0,006.0ms. In addition, Counting sort didn't increase at all when it climbed from 5000 to 10,000 elements (0,006.0ms). None of the other algorithms seem to be nearly as impressive. Consistent with recent literature it also demonstrated linear O(n) time, although the plot seems to reflect more O(log n) time. Counting sort is a non-comparison sort, and research has shown again and again that Counting Sort is typically faster than comparison based types such as Merge Sort and Heap Sort. As we have learned, non-comparison types like Counting sort are not limited to n log n time. However, a downside of Counting sort is that it cannot be implemented on arrays comprised of floats.

Like Counting sort, Merge sort also performed very fast across all its input size instances, albeit we see a seemingly notable change when it jumps from 2,500 to 5,000 elements, i.e. 0,024.0ms to 0,054.0ms. It struggles further when it encounters 10,000 elements by taking twice as long as when it handles 5,000, i.e. 0,117.9ms. That said, this is a minute additional demand on time resources relative to the increase in input size. Similarly, Heap sort was very fast across all its input size instances, almost identical to that of Merge sort. That said, Heap sort is an unstable algorithm and still not quite as fast. Furthermore, once both algorithms pass the n = 2500, they really do begin to strain notably, whereas Counting sort endeavors without a hitch. Nevertheless, while advanced recursive algorithms like Merge sort or Heap sort are difficult to implement, they still run very efficiently on data arrays, typically in O(n log n) steps.

By looking at the multiple algorithm chart, we can clearly infer how once n reaches around 2000 elements, Insertion and Bubble sort skyrocket in complexities of growth whilst Merge, Counting and Heap persevere in an ostensibly linear fashion. It seems that while Counting sort performs significantly better across all input sizes compared to the other four algorithms, Merge and Heap sort are still both notably faster than Insertion and Bubble, which performs with a far less impressive run time calibre. We should bare in mind that Counting sorts efficacy depends largely on the fact that it knows the array ranges and max value before being implemented, which was true in the case of this analysis. Following reflective consideration regarding the individual performances demonstrated by each algorithm in this analysis, a handful of tentative observations and caveats can be offered for the future selection and implementaion of sorting algorithms. For example:

  • For arrays less than 500 items, all five algorithms are perfectly acceptable.

  • Counting sort appears to be the most optimal algorithm across every input size instance.

  • Bubble sort should never be considered where n exceeds 500 elements.

  • When Bubble sort climbs from 2,500 to 5000 elements it takes approximately 5 times as long to sort.

  • When Bubble sort climbss from 5,000 to 10,000 elements it takes approximately 4 times as long to sort.

  • For arrays that are large, i.e. those that exceed n = 750, Insertion and Bubble sort should not be used.

  • Heap sort is impressive, but begins to lose its perfromance power, notably from 5,000 elements upward.

  • When input sizes exceed n = 2500, Counting sort should be chosen over Merge and Heap sort, provided the input range is known prior to implementation.

  • All algorithms perform equally comparable to their own rankings from 100 to 10,000 elements.

  • Incorporating a random seed into the benchmarking process may have negative effects on the validity of individual algorithm performance, and therefore is not advisable.

Reflection on project learning outcomes:

Conscious efforts were made to ensure internal validity. Here, best practice was made to avoid confounding factors and to keep conditions constant for each algorithm implementation and the timing of such. For example, not using a random seed for generating random arrays, and following the exact same coding protocol for each algorithm in isolation.

In general, the performace all five algorithms in this analysis was in-line with and corroborated most of what has been discussed in previous research literature. While this benchmarking process appeared to be an effective method of comparing how different algorithms perform, the main disadvantage is undoubtedly that the empirical data was almost entirely dependent on the machine it was obtained on. For example, it is true that notably different results can arise from running algorithms on alternative systems such as a high calibre desktop running on a Linux OS. Indeed, an empirical comparison like this one is that is limited if it is system-dependent.

Arguably, a more effective method of comparing algorithms is through their time complexity upper bound to guarantee that an algorithm will run at most in a certain time with order of magnitude O(f(n)), where n is the number of items in the array to be sorted, or in other words by performing a thorough asymptotic analysis. For example, while Insertion and Bubble sort have a worst-case runtime of O(n2), and Merge and Heap sort a worst-case of O(n log n), Counting sort has a worst, average and best-case of O(n). This means that, on average it will always be faster than the other four, and especially so if the list is sufficiently large [27]. Indeed, this is precisely what occurred with this current analysis. Nervertheless, a detailed comparison cannot be made using asymptotic analysis alone, for example, taking into account some algorithms have the same order of magnitude (e.g. Bubble and Insertion), and furthermore because a couple of the algorithms in this analysis even appeared to run faster than their well documented best-case run times when plotted. It can be thus be posited that a thorough empirical comparison can be used to afford more comparable data, as opposed to a purely asymptotic one.

Another limiting factor may have been the coding language used in this analysis. While Python was employed, a different language such as C, may have resulted in faster sorting due to C's compiled nature compared to Python's interpreted approach. Furthermore, some of the algorithms were recursive in nature, i.e. Heap and Merge sort. While they are admirably fast performers on small and very large arrays, it would be interesting to measure alternative iterative versions of these algorithms to see how well the would perform againsts already-iterative types like Insertion or Bubble sort, especially as these two algorithms displayed the slowest performance times.

The algorithms in this investigation sorted purely random arrays generated by shuffling sequences of numbers via Numpy's randint method. While the analysis ostensibly shows reliable times necessary to sort these random arrays, most of the time this is not an entirely random process, instead it is psuedo-random. Furthermore, oftentimes these lists can be generated in an almost-sorted like fashion before an algorithm is used on them. If arrays of this type are fed in, they can result in the analyst offerring incorrect interpretations, completely unbeknownst to him or her. It could be posited, that arrays presented in reverse sorted order, or arrays with many duplicate elements may be used to lead to a more robust and valid evaluation of determining the optimal algorithm and preventing erroneous interpretations [27].

From an empirical perspective, one of the things that is most fascinating is the phenomena of input size influencing running time, resulting in each algorithm displaying its own unique growth order signature. The relationship here most definitely appears to be a causal one. It is intrguing how some algorithms appear to be custom built for handling larger arrays with ease, remaining unaffected as n increases dramatically, while others fall at the first hurdle. Indeed, by their individual designs, some algorithms possess far more formidable qualities.

If you watch a visualization of different sorting algorithms working on a data set, it becomes very obvious, very quickly that some are far better than others. Where one takes seconds to finish, another will take minutes even with small data sets. Fortunately, we don't need to see the algorithm at work to know if it can get the job done properly or if it will collapse under the weight of input. Indeed, that is what Big O notation is for, and it can tell us at glance if the time complexity of an algorithm will take minutes or even hours. However, the truth is that sorting algorithm research has been at an impasse for some time now. Research has shown that scientists actually can't just "come up" with better sorting algorithms. This is not for lack of trying, the entire field of computer science is all about trying to find the most efficient algorithm for a given problem, but sometimes they just don't know the math, and this includes Field's Medal winners who've come up against problems like the O(2n) and O(n!) time complexities and got nowhere trying to improve on them. Indeed, there exists a whole catalog of problems (including sorting problems) that mathematicians and computer scientists simply don't have the math or computational resources to solve yet. These problems have created salient choking points and inefficiencies in businesses, scientific research and similar sectors. Lucrative prizes have even been offered to anyone who can solve these problems which have been sitting around for decades. Classical computers can't solve them efficiently either because every instruction they are given must be essentially be completed before it can start the next one on a schedule. While multicore systems and multiprocessors speed things up, it is nonetheless extremely difficult to make the operations go faster [29].

While this impasse is a hard fact of modern day computer science, algorithm performance research is still one of the most exciting, and genuinely fascinating fields out there. There is much to learn from and build on. Through relentless trial and error experimentation, efforts to improve and design new algorithms is paramount in the modern world. Algorithmic research is central to the field of data science and many computer applications which are contingent upon it, e.g. designing decision making software or predicting various phenomena using machine learning techniques. Indeed, the science of sorting algorithms is fertile and exciting, and perhaps that is why it is so relevant, albeit for now, notably difficult to advance.
